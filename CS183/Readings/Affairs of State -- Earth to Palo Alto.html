Earth to Palo Alto

By George M. Kraw
Special to The Recorder
October 31, 2001

What would you do with a 3-year-old company that has never turned an annual profit, is on track to lose a quarter billion dollars and whose recent SEC filings warn that its services might be used for money laundering and financial fraud?

If you were the managers and venture capitalists behind Palo Alto's PayPal, you'd take it public. And that is what they hope to do in an $80 million offering that will test the limits of investor tolerance and financial market gullibility.

In an era where international terrorism is paid for in the shadows of the world financial system, the United States needs a publicly traded electronic money service that operates on the cusp of bank regulatory laws as much as it does an anthrax epidemic. But Silicon Valley VCs -- along with the lawyers, consultants, accountants and other professional service providers dependent on the VC deal stream -- want this and other IPOs to succeed.

The economic shakeout had destroyed investor confidence in Valley tech firms even before the events of Sept. 11. Companies are now facing a skeptical market environment that emphasizes results rather than promises. Famous names invoking memories of past hot issues no longer cut it. Loudcloud -- a profitless Internet operations service provider headed by Netscape founder Marc Andreessen -- went out at $6 a share in March in a pared-down deal. The stock's price has been more than halved, in spite of Andreessen's massive share buybacks.

Even some of the Valley's biggest promoters belatedly recognize the corrosive, corrupting influence that the get-rich- quick, get-it-all-now schemes have had on the entrepreneurial culture.

"There's a battle going on for the soul of the entrepreneur," chastened venture capitalist John Doerr told the San Jose Mercury News earlier this year. "It's a contest between the missionaries and the mercenaries."

Doerr says the contest is between those who want to use businesses to create and apply revolutionary technologies and those who see businesses as a means to a financial end.

But these days it's more basic than that. After Sept. 11, responsible Americans see themselves as having civic responsibilities that extend beyond lining their pockets.

PayPal is hardly the worst example of Valley excess -- it's just another child of the arrogant age -- but it's the wrong deal at the wrong time.

"I like to think of us as being at the forefront of financial liberation," Chief Executive Officer Peter Thiel, who likely won't be landing in Kandahar, told Wired magazine earlier this year. "But if we didn't do it, someone else would."

The financial liberation that Thiel is talking about is the ability to e-mail cash without complying with federal banking rules. Representatives from PayPal claim that requiring its customers to disclose their Social Security numbers would violate their privacy. "No one knows what defines a bank," Thiel told Wired. "There's no clear standard."

Initially, the company provided an encrypted means for sending funds back and forth between Palm Pilots and other hand-held devices. This service might eventually have had some appeal to drug dealers and domestic terrorists, but it never caught on with the public at large and was abandoned.

Most of PayPal's current business is as a settlement service for eBay auction customers, allowing sellers to be paid without having to wait for checks. It's now trying to diversify into non-auction related transactions, where its ambiguous legal status gives it an advantage over rivals that are owned by banks and which must comply with federal banking regulations. But those advantages make PayPal's services vulnerable to criminal abuse.

Apparently, there was insufficient adult supervision around PayPal to prevent its Sept. 28 S-1 filing with the SEC.

The commercialization of professions and its attendant financial pressures here and elsewhere have destroyed the distance most professionals traditionally maintained from their clients. The detached professional offering disinterested counsel has been transformed into the business valet, caught in a web of complex continuing relationships and charged chiefly with doing the client's bidding and achieving its goals.

Valley professionals, especially those in the legal profession dependent on venture deals, might ask themselves what are the obligations of citizenship, especially for a licensed professional in a dangerous age. They might want to raise other issues in their partnership meetings: "Say, was I put on this earth to pimp for every irresponsible ambition that meets the firm's representation requirements?" Or: "Isn't it swell that we can sit around arguing how to suck more money out of the economy -- no matter the consequences -- while our cleaning women's kids fight our wars and protect our cities?"

When things were going grandly, local lawyers were eager to proclaim the new age of lawyering, where traditional notions of civic, professional and personal responsibility would be passe, or could be salved by a contribution to a pro bono program. That nonsense is blessedly in the past.

But in Palo Alto and elsewhere, there's still the attorney version of John Doerr's question:

"Counselor, are you merely a mercenary?" Let each lawyer answer for him or herself.
